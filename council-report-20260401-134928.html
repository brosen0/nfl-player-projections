LLM Council Verdict

Question: What is the single most critical limitation of the NFL player projection system?
Date: April 1, 2026 · 5 advisors · 5 peer reviews · 1 chairman synthesis

The Recommendation

The root cause is most likely one of two things:

  • A data leakage or pipeline bug in the 2025 backtest — explains the systematic shift, the uniform direction of bias, and the catastrophic year-over-year degradation.
  • Temporal overfitting to regime-specific correlations — explains why 100+ features and rolling windows produce a model that cannot generalize across scoring environments.

Audit for (1) first. If the pipeline is clean, strip to a small set of causal features, accept a lower but stable R², and add a sanity-check circuit breaker. The component decomposition approach (predict targets/yards/attempts, assemble FP) is the right long-term architecture but is a Phase 2 effort. Do not pursue distributional prediction until point estimates are unbiased.

The One Thing to Do First

Audit the 2025 backtest pipeline for data leakage. Manually trace feature values for 3–5 players in Week 1 of 2025 and verify no statistic from 2025 or late 2024 (after training cutoff) contaminates any rolling average, lag feature, or engineered variable. If leakage is found, fix it and re-run. If the pipeline is clean, the non-stationarity diagnosis is confirmed.

Blind Spots the Council Caught

The peer review surfaced the most important finding: every advisor jumped to architectural explanations, but nobody investigated whether the 2025 backtest itself is broken. A +3.6 systematic shift across all positions is consistent with a pipeline bug — season-end stats leaking into rolling feature calculations during backtesting, or future information contaminating 2025 feature construction.

The Expansionist's suggestion (distributional prediction) was unanimously identified as addressing presentation while ignoring that predictions are systematically wrong. Well-calibrated intervals around a biased center are still wrong.

Full Advisor Responses

The Contrarian Strongest

The fatal flaw: the model memorizes regime-specific correlations that don't transfer across seasons. Training R² of 0.22–0.60, then 2025 out-of-sample R² of -0.685 — that's a model that learned nothing generalizable. A negative R² means you'd be better off predicting the mean.

100+ features reduced to ~15 via VIF is a red herring of rigor. VIF removes multicollinearity, not overfitting to temporal patterns. Rolling averages, lag features, and recency decay all encode recent history as if it predicts the future, but NFL production is dominated by role changes, scheme changes, coaching changes, and injury cascades that no backward-looking feature captures.

The single most critical limitation: treating a non-stationary, regime-switching process as a stationary regression problem with temporal weighting.

Fix: Strip to 5–10 causal features (opportunity share, snap %, team pace). Drop rolling/lag beyond 3-week windows. Accept R² ceiling of 0.15–0.20 out-of-sample. Build around calibrated uncertainty rather than point prediction accuracy.

Peer review: Unanimously selected as strongest response by all 5 reviewers.

The First Principles Thinker

You don't have a modeling problem. You have a prediction target problem. Fantasy points are dominated by touchdowns — high-variance, low-autocorrelation events. A WR catching 6 passes for 70 yards scores ~10 PPR. Add one TD and it's 16. That TD is nearly coin-flip predictable.

R² of 0.233 isn't "decent" — it's the ceiling. The 2025 collapse isn't a bug; it's what happens when you overfit to predictable variance in your training window.

Fix: Stop predicting fantasy points directly. Predict stable components (targets, receptions, yards, rushing attempts) then assemble. Each component has higher autocorrelation and lower TD contamination. The two-stage approach for RB was the right instinct — apply it everywhere.

The Outsider

The model doesn't know it's wrong. It predicts every WR will score 5.6 more than actual with no circuit breaker. The architecture reads like a system designed to be impressive rather than right. Five ML models ensembled, 100+ features, utilization-based two-stage for some positions but not others. Every technique sounds good in a writeup, but the result is a TE predicted at 12 when reality is 6.6.

The absence of post-prediction sanity checking. Before any prediction leaves the system, compare against trivially simple baselines: last season's average, position average, Vegas implied totals.

Fix: Build a calibration gate. Compare ensemble output against naive baseline. If deviation exceeds threshold, blend back toward baseline. Two-day fix that catches the entire 2025 collapse.

The Executor

The model memorizes historical scoring environments instead of learning portable relationships. The +3.6 over-prediction means the model learned "WR with these metrics scores X" based on 2006–2024, but 2025's scoring shifted downward.

Three concrete steps: (1) Predict rank-order or z-scores within position-week, not raw FP — sidesteps the calibration problem. (2) Post-prediction calibration via isotonic regression on a rolling 3-week window. (3) Drop training data before 2018 — pre-2018 NFL is a different sport.

Start with rank-order. It's the structural fix.

The Expansionist Weakest

The system should predict distributions, not point estimates. Fantasy value is about probability of outperforming cost basis. Shift to distributional prediction: intervals become the product, bias matters less, you unlock DFS/draft optimization.

Fix: Quantile regression, NGBoost, LightGBM with quantile loss. Output P10/P50/P90.

Peer review: Unanimously identified as weakest — distributional prediction on a systematically biased model just gives well-calibrated intervals around the wrong center.

Peer Review Highlights

Unanimous strongest: The Contrarian (Response A). Correctly identifies treating non-stationarity as stationarity. The +3.6 bias is exactly what you get when temporal features encode a 2024 scoring regime that didn't persist.

Unanimous weakest: The Expansionist (Response C). Sidesteps the diagnosis entirely. Quantile regression on a model that's +3.6 wrong gives biased quantiles. It answers a different question.

Unanimous blind spot: Nobody investigated data leakage in the backtest pipeline. A +3.6 systematic shift is consistent with features encoding future information during backtesting. Season-end stats leaking into rolling calculations, or target variables informing feature scaling. Audit the plumbing before redesigning the house.
